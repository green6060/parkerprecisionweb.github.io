Great Explorers in the Deep

Great White Sharks named Caitlan and Thomas are tagged for success.

See Clinton dissect a great white shark.

A great white shark was retrieved dead by a commercial fisher off the Taranaki coast on Monday 16th November 2015.
 
With support from the Department on Conservation and Ngāti Tairi, Oākura Pā, Taranaki, it was sent to the Auckland War Memorial Museum to be necropsied by Clinton Duffy, taking measurements and then dissecting the shark to collect tissues and parasites. The jaws were removed and returned to Ngāti Tairi, Oākura Pā, Taranaki.
 
Clinton’s research on great whites is supported by New Zealand Oil & Gas as a contribution to better scientific understanding of our marine environment.

 

 

Sometimes the journey is as important as the destination.

So it has been in recent weeks for young Caitlan and Thomas as they glide in—or perhaps near—the azure waters of Kaipara Harbour.

It was here in this estuary complex on the northwestern side of the North Island these two beautiful monsters were tagged this year by Clinton Duffy and his fellow researchers.

 

The sharks measure in at 2.1m and 2.2m respectively, and tagging them was a lengthy affair too.

Here, as elsewhere over the past decade, the sharks were lured to the side of the boat with a mix of tuna oil and minced tuna. When they got close enough, a long pole was then used to stab the tag into each of them, injecting it under the skin in the muscle below the dorsal fin, fitting each with a GPS wildlife-tracking device.

So the journey of discovery begins. The scholars give the marine creatures alphanumeric codes and nicknames. Nicknames are fun and easier to remember, explains Clinton, a shark scientist with the Department of Conservation whose work is actively supported by New Zealand Oil & Gas.

 

Nicknames also create a bit of an extra bond during the tracking process, during which data on light levels, temperature and depth experienced by the shark are transmitted to an ARGOS satellite, which emails the data back to the scientists, which can go on for up to nine months.

Charting how great white sharks live is also the academic life of Clinton, who also is the official keeper of New Zealand’s shark attack records.

What he knows is impressive. But what this explorer doesn’t know is significant, too.

The ocean is vast, much of it uncovered, and so is what we still don’t understand about great white sharks, says Dr Duffy, whose lifelong passion was originally spurred as a child frolicking on the beaches of the Wairarapa coast.

Today it is part of a wider research project that began in earnest in 2005.

Initially, he began reporting on population figures and migratory behaviour of the protected species to assess how many sharks were being killed in fishing nets and lines.

Until the research project nothing was known about great white sharks in New Zealand, he explains. Within two years the animal had become protected.

His work adds verifiable information on how effective the conservation status appears to be, as well as valuable insight into fishing-industry activity.  

It adds to the worldwide store of scientific knowledge, too.

That’s important because the great white shark is a versatile and adaptable creature, as much so as it is an endangered species, and what might be discovered in New Zealand will often throw new light on similar work being undertaken in foreign waters.

Trophy hunting, shark control programmes, accidental capture in gill nets and vulnerability to over-fishing are all contributors to this fearsome and awe-inspiring marine creature's demise.

It also—perhaps—leads to Clinton’s marine friends turning up in surprising places.

“I’ve tagged two or three of them just off the runway at Auckland Airport,” he laughs. “And that’s not the only place. I’ve been sitting in boats looking at sheep and cows grazing in paddocks on Stewart Island or the Chatham Islands … clear blue water, waves crashing, albatrosses flapping overhead … and lo and behold, one will turn up.”

Right now there still isn’t a lot to report on Caitlan and Thomas.  Not much has been heard from the sharks, just a fix every other day.   

Thomas has been drifting up and down the Kaipara River channel between South Head and Aotea cliffs on the Argos website. Claudia, meanwhile, has been close to shore, north of the harbour off Lake Wairere.

The exploratory journey continues.

The Tagger's Tools

From Clinton Duffy’s briefcase, here are some essential items for those looking to spend their business day tagging great white sharks.

Argos:

Argos is a location and data collection system that uses low earth-orbiting satellites to detect and monitor a variety of transmitters (http://www.argos-system.org). In operation since 1978, it was created as a collaborative project between the French space agency CNES and US government agencies NOAA and NASA.

SPOT tags:

Wildlife Computers SPOT tags transmit to the Argos system to provide data on horizontal movement. The tags also include a temperature sensor that can be used to collect information on temperature preferences of the study animal in the form of time-at-temperature histograms.  The SPOT5-196E, pictured here mounted on Caitlan’s dorsal fin, has an expected life of 280 days.

Mk10 PAT and MiniPAT tags:

Wildlife Computers PAT tags are pop-up archival transmitting (PAT) tags, also known as a PSAT tags.  They resemble a hand-held microphone.  The tag is attached by way of a nylon tether to a dart, which is injected under the skin of the shark using a tag pole.  (Clinton uses the Domeier plastic umbrella dart.)  In the case of the sharks tagged in Kaipara Harbour, the tags were programmed to archive data on depth, temperature and ambient light-levels every 60 seconds throughout a nine month deployment.  The attached image shows an Mk10 PAT tag deployed on a 3.1 m male white shark at Ariel Bank off Gisborne on 9 September 2009. (Sharks are often double tagged with a SPOT and a PAT tag in case the SPOT tag fails for some reason.)

About Clinton Duffy

Clinton Duffy is an explorer. He's a marine biologist and scientist who understands the sensitivities of marine life after many years studying great white sharks.

New Zealand Oil & Gas approached him in 2012 to offer support for his research, which is the subject of his Doctorate at the University of Auckland.

"It’s a population of sharks that we know very little about," Clinton says. His study will help scientists understand conservation management requirements for these great explorers.

“We developed a proposal to satellite tag as many sharks as we could with a hope that we could track those sharks along the outer coast and then potentially fly aerial surveys in the areas that the sharks were transmitting from to see what they might be feeding on.

"What I am hoping to achieve from this research project is to develop a much better understanding about the habitats the Great White sharks are using around New Zealand.

"Ultimately I want to come up with a population estimate based on the photo identification work that we are doing.”

Clinton has been assisted for many years by Kina Scollay, a highly specialised underwater video camera operator who himself has endured many risky encounters with the great white.

“White sharks are extremely efficient swimmers," Kina says.

"They’re ambush predators who typically surprise their prey with a rapid burst of speed. When they are not hunting, when they are just cruising, they are extremely efficient swimmers and use very little energy.

"They are a very large fish, the largest females on record are estimated to be between seven and seven and a half metres in length.

"Females are much larger than males, typically twice the weight of a male that is the same length. I examined one that was caught by accident off the Raglan coast in 2005. She was four point seven metres long and weighed 1.6 tonnes and was immature. "

The maximum age of the white sharks is poorly estimated. Experts believed they lived to a similar age as the mako shark - about 40 years. But then a large male great white shark was recently aged at 76 years.
A male matures at around 7 – 10 years old whereas females mature much older at around 18-20 years.
